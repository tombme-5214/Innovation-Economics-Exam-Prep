1. What is the Economics of Innovation?

The economics of innovation uses economic tools to study how new ideas are created, commercialised and diffused, and how these processes affect growth, productivity and welfare.

It spans microeconomics (firms, inventors, households), macroeconomics (growth and development), and economic policy (how governments should respond to market failures in innovation).

2. Why Innovation Matters

Innovation and technological progress are seen as key drivers of economic development and wealth, explaining more than half of historical economic growth through productivity gains and new forms of capital.

Innovation improves the effectiveness of physical capital, complements labour, raises the returns to human capital, and can generate self‑sustaining, endogenous growth.

3. Definitions and Types of Innovation

Following guidelines such as the Oslo Manual, innovation covers new or significantly improved products and processes, new markets or sources of supply, and changes in organisational structures or strategies.

We distinguish between product innovation, process innovation, and “soft” or organisational innovation, all of which involve changes in knowledge and its applications.

4. Key Thinkers

Several classic economists placed innovation at the centre of their analysis:

  • Smith: Division of labour encourages inventions and raises the wealth of nations.
  • Ricardo: New machinery responds to labour costs and affects wages and employment.
  • Mill: Inventions are central to progress but may not immediately benefit all.
  • Marx: Capitalism constantly revolutionises production through innovation and competition.
  • Schumpeter: Innovation drives “creative destruction,” reshaping industries from within.
  • Nelson & Winter: Organisations innovate by breaking routinised behaviour through learning.

5. Scientists, Technology and Knowledge

Scientists create and disseminate knowledge, often as a public good that generates positive externalities, while firms transform this knowledge into technology through R&D, protected partly by intellectual property.

This separation between knowledge creation and commercial application underpins the innovation system and motivates policies around funding research, IP rights and support for R&D.
